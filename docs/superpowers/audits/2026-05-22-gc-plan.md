## 2. EU Accessibility Act — the detailed answer

### 2.1 Does it apply?

**Confidence: 70% it does NOT apply at the moment you remain a solo founder in stealth with no users.** Reasoning:

- **Scope (Art. 2):** the EAA covers products and services placed on the EU market after 28 June 2025, INCLUDING "consumer banking services," "e-commerce services," and "services providing access to audiovisual media." A standalone free financial-forecasting tool sits in a grey zone: it is **not** "consumer banking" (you are not a credit institution, you take no deposits, you execute no payments today), and it is arguably **not** "e-commerce" until you actually transact. The narrowest reading: you are out of scope until PayPal goes live.
- **Microenterprise exemption (Art. 4(5)):** explicit carve-out for enterprises <10 employees AND <€2M annual turnover (or balance-sheet total). A solo founder pre-revenue qualifies textually. **Important nuance** the founder should not over-rely on: the exemption applies to **services** but **not to products** (Art. 4(5) wording). ProFinanceCast as a web service is on the service side, so the exemption is available — but the moment any part of the product is sold or distributed as a downloadable artifact, the analysis changes.
- **Pre-launch / stealth:** the EAA regulates products "placed on the market" and services "provided to consumers." A stealth product with zero users is, on any sensible reading, neither placed nor provided. The risk is not the law applying retroactively — it is launching without compliance and being caught at user #1.

### 2.2 The traps to watch for

These are the conditions under which the "doesn't apply" answer flips:

1. **You accept your first paying EU user.** The microenterprise exemption for services still applies, but national regulators in DE, FR, IT have signalled willingness to treat any commercial activity as triggering at least the accessibility-statement obligation.
2. **You grow past 10 staff OR €2M turnover OR €2M balance-sheet.** Single revenue or hiring trigger flips it.
3. **You integrate payments and you become the merchant-of-record.** Now you are unambiguously a "service" in the EAA service catalogue (e-commerce service per Annex I, point d).
4. **You receive institutional funding.** Pre-seed angels = probably fine; a €1M seed round can push you over balance-sheet thresholds depending on accounting treatment.
5. **A member state has gold-plated transposition.** France, Germany, Italy, and Spain have all transposed with local variations. If you target one specific country, check that country's transposition law — not the directive.

### 2.3 If it applies, what's the timeline and cost?

- **Enforcement timeline:** member states enforce. Most have grace periods through 2026–2027 for existing services. New services launched after 28 June 2025 are immediately in scope.
- **Compliance cost (rough order):** WCAG 2.1 AA audit + remediation €5K–€25K for a single-page-app of this size. Accessibility statement: a one-page document, near-zero cost.
- **Worst-case penalty:** member-state fines run €5K–€1M; the more painful exposure is a **removal-from-market order** (national regulator orders the service taken down until compliant). For a stealth product with near-zero users this is reputationally fatal.

### 2.4 Verdict

**Defer the formal EAA compliance program by 3–6 months.** But:

- Ship WCAG 2.1 AA technical audit tonight as defence-in-depth (see §6).
- Add a one-page accessibility statement before user #1 (template: see EU Commission templates, takes 30 minutes).
- Document the microenterprise determination in a one-page memo and keep it with the corporate records.
- Re-trigger this analysis the moment ANY of the five traps in §2.2 fires.

## 3. GDPR — the live wire (more urgent than the EAA)

**This is what you should actually be worried about tonight.** GDPR applies the moment a single EU-resident person hits a login form. The risk profile is different from the EAA — much lower individual-incident penalty, but vastly higher likelihood (basically certain).

### 3.1 The five non-negotiable items before user #1

1. **Privacy notice.** Plain-language, on the landing page, identifying you as controller, your contact, lawful bases per processing purpose, retention, third-country transfers (Vercel = US), data-subject rights, supervisory-authority right-to-complain.
2. **Lawful basis decided for each data category.** Account email = contract (Art. 6(1)(b)). Analytics = consent or legitimate interest (depends on cookies). Marketing emails = consent.
3. **DPA with Vercel.** Vercel publishes one; you must accept it explicitly. Same for any sub-processor (Plausible, Stripe, PayPal, Sentry, Postmark, etc.).
4. **Cookie / tracker audit.** Anything beyond strictly-necessary needs prior consent under ePrivacy. Vercel Analytics, Sentry session replay, Plausible (debatable — Plausible argues no-cookies-no-consent-needed, regulators are split).
5. **Schrems II transfer mechanism.** Vercel = US processor. You need Standard Contractual Clauses (Vercel provides) + Transfer Impact Assessment (one-page TIA at this scale is sufficient).

### 3.2 What you can defer to later

- DPO appointment (not required at your scale).
- Full ROPA (Art. 30) — required, but a one-page-spreadsheet version is defensible for a solo founder.
- DPIA — only required for high-risk processing (you're probably not there until you add behavioural scoring or profiling).

### 3.3 The single most-likely-to-bite scenario

Founder ships, a single EU user files an Art. 15 access request, founder has no process to respond within 1 month, complaint goes to the user's national DPA, DPA opens a low-priority investigation. Most-likely outcome: a reprimand and a process improvement order. Worst case: published decision, reputational damage at launch.

**Mitigation:** a one-line privacy@profinancecast.com inbox and a written "what we do when we get an access request" SOP (template = 45 minutes). Do this before user #1.

## 4. The consumer-finance sleeper risk

This is the regulation question the founder did NOT ask but I'm flagging because it's where solo-founder fintech projects most commonly get surprised.

### 4.1 What's the worry

A "personal-finance forecasting tool" sits adjacent to three regulated activities:

- **Investment advice** (MiFID II, national equivalents). Personalised recommendation regarding a specific financial instrument = regulated. A retirement projection that says "you should buy more equities" can be read as advice in some member states.
- **Financial promotion** (UK FCA rules, FR AMF rules, DE BaFin rules). Anything that promotes or could be construed as promoting a financial product to consumers. Different rules per state.
- **Insurance Distribution Directive** if you ever cover insurance products.

### 4.2 Likelihood it bites ProFinanceCast as currently scoped

**Low (~15–25%) IF the product strictly outputs cash-flow projections, scenario modelling, and education** — and explicitly disclaims advice, recommendation, or suitability assessment. **Higher (~40–60%) IF the product:**

- Recommends specific products, allocations, or strategies (e.g. "you should allocate 60/40").
- Mentions specific named instruments (specific ETFs, specific banks).
- Provides a "risk score" or "suitability" output of any kind.
- Charges for personalised output (the act of charging tips many member states into the regulated zone).

### 4.3 Cheap protective measures (do tonight)

1. **Site-wide disclaimer in the footer:** "ProFinanceCast provides forecasting and educational tools only. It is not a regulated financial-services firm, does not provide investment advice, recommendations, or suitability assessments, and does not consider your individual circumstances. Consult a regulated financial adviser before making any financial decision."
2. **Avoid these words in product copy:** *recommend, advise, suitable for you, you should, allocate, optimal portfolio, risk score (when applied to a person rather than to a portfolio behaviour).*
3. **Use these words instead:** *forecast, project, model, scenario, illustrate, simulate.*
4. **Audit the existing "vintage-ledger" hero copy and the Pro page for any phrase that crosses the line.** This is a 30-minute pass.

### 4.4 What to take to outside counsel

A short memo: "we provide cash-flow projections and scenario tools; we don't recommend specific instruments; we don't take custody of assets; we don't execute trades; we have a not-financial-advice disclaimer." Counsel can confirm in 30 minutes whether that fact-pattern keeps you out of the regulated perimeter in your target launch jurisdictions. Cost: €500–€1500 for the memo opinion. **Worth every euro before user #1.**

## 5. The "vintage-ledger / European" positioning — does it create jurisdiction?

**Short answer: yes, materially, but not catastrophically.**

Under **Brussels I-bis Regulation Art. 17–19** and **Rome I Regulation Art. 6**, a business is treated as "directing activities" to an EU member state — and therefore must accept that state's consumer-protection law and courts — when there is evidence the business intentionally targets that state's consumers. Evidence the courts have accepted:

- Top-level domain (.eu, .pt, .de, .fr).
- Currency (EUR pricing).
- Language (Portuguese, French, German content).
- Imagery and cultural references that evoke specific member states.
- Phone numbers, addresses, or contact persons in member states.

**ProFinanceCast presents:** EUR sample user, Portugal-specific sample, "Mediterranean dawn / Lisbon Alfama" hero imagery, "quiet European forecasting house" brand voice. Under any reasonable reading, this is **directing activities to EU consumers, specifically including Portugal**.

### 5.1 What this changes

- **Choice-of-law clauses in your ToS are partially unenforceable** for EU consumers. You can pick US/Delaware law, but mandatory EU consumer protections still apply.
- **Choice-of-forum clauses are partially unenforceable.** EU consumers can sue you in their home country regardless of what your ToS say.
- **GDPR territorial scope (Art. 3(2)) is unambiguously triggered** — even with zero EU users, the "directing" alone suffices.
- **EAA territorial scope is plausibly triggered** the moment you "provide" the service to an EU consumer.

### 5.2 What this does NOT change

- You don't have to incorporate in the EU.
- You don't suddenly owe VAT (until you transact).
- You don't trigger MiFID II just by being EU-coded.

### 5.3 The strategic question for the founder

You have two coherent positions:

- **Position A (recommended):** lean into EU-first. The brand is European, the user is European, the compliance burden is the cost of that positioning. Budget for it. Outside counsel in the EU. EUR pricing. WCAG 2.1 AA. GDPR-clean from day one. The brand wins because compliance IS the product story for a "quiet European forecasting house."
- **Position B:** strip the European coding, position as US-first, defer EU launch by 6–12 months. This means new hero photography, new sample user, new copy. Significant brand cost.

**Mixed positions (EU-coded but US-compliant) are the worst-of-both-worlds** — you've taken on the jurisdictional burden without claiming the brand benefit. Pick one explicitly and document the decision.

## 6. The WCAG 2.1 AA question — ship tonight or defer?

**Ship it tonight.** Here's why, even though §2 says EAA probably doesn't apply yet:

| Reason | Cost of shipping tonight | Cost of deferring |
|---|---|---|
| WCAG 2.1 AA is the technical baseline EVERY EU accessibility regime references, regardless of EAA | 2–6 hours of audit + remediation work | If EAA applies sooner than expected, retrofit cost is 5–10x |
| WCAG is also the de-facto baseline for US ADA Title III lawsuits — and you have a US-facing surface even if EU-positioned | Same | Single ADA demand letter = €5K–€25K in settlement + counsel costs |
| Accessible code = better SEO + Lighthouse score + screen-reader users + keyboard users | Net positive | Lost users |
| If counsel later says EAA applies, you already have the artifact | Saves €5K–€25K | Costs €5K–€25K of remediation under deadline |
| Solo founder, near-zero users, code surface is small | Cheapest moment in the company's life to do this | Every additional feature multiplies remediation surface |

**The defence-in-depth principle.** Compliance posture is built from overlapping cheap controls, not one expensive perfect control. Shipping WCAG 2.1 AA tonight costs you a few hours and gives you simultaneous coverage against the EAA, ADA, future state-level laws, and the brand promise of "considered, careful, European." Deferring saves nothing meaningful.

**One caveat:** ship the audit + fixes, but DON'T add an "ADA Compliant" or "EAA Compliant" badge or claim. A claim of compliance you don't formally maintain is worse than no claim. A quiet accessibility statement (template in §2.4) describing your conformance level (e.g., "WCAG 2.1 AA, self-assessed, last reviewed 2026-05-22") is the correct register.

## 8. Kill-switch playbook — the regulator-knocks-tomorrow scenario

If an EU regulator (DPA, accessibility authority, financial-services authority, consumer-protection authority) sends a letter or opens an inquiry tomorrow, here is the cheapest defensive minimum you can deploy in 24 hours. **None of this is a substitute for engaging counsel within those same 24 hours — but it limits the damage while counsel is engaged.**

### Hour 0–2: Triage

- Read the letter carefully. Note: regulator, statutory authority cited, deadline, requested information, designated case officer.
- Do NOT respond yet. Do NOT panic-delete anything (spoliation risk).
- Engage outside counsel by hour 4. Counsel takes the response; you don't.

### Hour 2–8: Defensive deploys (in priority order)

1. **Privacy notice live and accurate** (if not already): template, lawful basis, contact email, data-subject-rights statement. Public URL `/privacy`.
2. **Accessibility statement live**: WCAG 2.1 AA conformance level, contact for accessibility feedback, last-reviewed date. Public URL `/accessibility`.
3. **Not-financial-advice disclaimer** in the footer and on any output page.
4. **Geo-block or rate-limit the targeted member state IF counsel advises** (this is a counsel call — geo-blocking the complainant's state can look like obstruction).
5. **Snapshot the entire site** (version-controlled commit + a static HTML mirror) at time-of-letter to prove what was live. Forensic evidence.
6. **Inbox capture**: any data-subject access request or accessibility-feedback message gets forwarded to a single email address counsel monitors.

### Hour 8–24: The strategic minimum

1. **Pull the product offline if counsel advises.** A 30-day take-down + relaunch with full compliance is cheaper than fighting a regulator while live. Solo-founder near-zero-users = take-down cost is near-zero. This is the genuinely-cheapest kill-switch and it is severely underused by founders who become emotionally attached to staying live.
2. **Send the regulator a holding response** drafted by counsel: acknowledging receipt, noting the company is a microenterprise, requesting a 30-day extension (almost always granted for first contact), committing to a substantive response by [date].
3. **Document every decision in a single timeline document.** Regulators reward visible process; they punish silence.

### The 24-hour cheapest-compliant-minimum stack

If you literally had to make the product compliant overnight: take the product offline, replace the landing page with a holding page ("ProFinanceCast is briefly offline while we improve accessibility and privacy controls. Back soon."), keep nothing live but a contact email and a privacy notice. That is, almost always, the right move at this scale.
